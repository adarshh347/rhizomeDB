Bambach's Element reads Heidegger's late turn to poetry not as a literary hobby but as a different *way to do philosophy* — language taken as fundamentally poetic, and thinking re-grounded on that basis. The spine of the Introduction is the diagnosis of an age in which calculative, cybernetic thinking has become the only thinking we recognize.

<describe>
Heidegger's charge: under the dominance of the sciences our thinking "will soon be determined and regulated by the new fundamental science called cybernetics." The "end of philosophy" is the completion of metaphysics as nihilism — the steerable arrangement of a scientific-technological world. Against this he sets *Andenken*: not mere remembrance of the past but a "recollective thinking ahead," turned toward what is coming.
</describe>

<anchor>
What is most thought-provoking in our thought-provoking time is that we are still not yet thinking. To address that deficit, thinking must take a *leap* (Sprung) into being — "a leap into the groundless from the habitual ground upon which, in each case, beings are."
</anchor>

The whole machinery of the Introduction turns on this: ordinary philosophy gives grounds, but the thinking Heidegger wants is a *leap of departure* (Absprung) out of metaphysics — and the runway for that leap is poetry, specifically Hölderlin.

<assert>
Poetry is not language's handmaiden. <sutra>"Poetry never takes language as a raw material present at hand; rather, poetry itself first makes language possible." The essence of language must be understood out of the essence of poetry, and not the other way around.</sutra>
</assert>

<reveal>
Stop seeing language as a tool the human being *has* and masters — start seeing it as that which *has* the human being. "Language is therefore not something that the human being has, but the reverse: that which has the human being. What the human being is — we are a conversation." Mastery of language is the master-illusion; the human only speaks insofar as it *corresponds* to language and heeds its appeal.
</reveal>

This is the load-bearing inversion of the whole section, and it has a second face in the ethics material. Heidegger reroutes *ethos* away from rules and values back to its Greek root.

<anchor>
*Ethos* means dwelling, sojourn — *Aufenthalt*, the human being's abode in the midst of beings as a whole. Ethics in its originary sense is not a doctrine of virtue or values (those are "the consequence, mutation, and aberration of the concealed originary meaning") but the question of how we dwell. <sutra>Ethics begins in uncertainty and aporia, where the rule does not fit the case and calculation cannot fit the circumstance.</sutra>
</anchor>

<chamatkara>
The uncanny turn in the dwelling argument: at the very heart of our abode, where we believe ourselves most at home, "there perdures a strange otherness... It is precisely this monstrousness that continues to haunt us in our haunts, threatening to dislodge us from our lodgings." The home is built out of the unhomely. Trakl's line lands the flash: *"The soul is a stranger on the earth."*
</chamatkara>

<chamatkara>
Hölderlin's bridge over the abyss, and the verse Heidegger cites endlessly — "But where there is danger, / There grows also that which saves" — recast as the danger of all dangers: not the atomic threat itself but *the danger of not even recognizing the danger*. The peril is precisely the inability to see the peril.
</chamatkara>

<correlate>
The leap (Sprung / Absprung) out of accumulated ground — "no amount of grounds produces it; you suddenly stand elsewhere" — maps cleanly onto *chamatkāra* in the Abhinavaguptan/Kashmir-Śaiva line: the sudden flash where the familiar becomes strange and a new register of seeing opens at once. Not forced — both are anti-incremental theories of insight, where the decisive movement is a break in continuity rather than a sum of steps, and both treat that break as the *real* beginning of thought. This extends the human note's already-marked Heidegger↔chamatkāra bridge to a fresh, independent passage (the Absprung), so it is corroboration rather than repetition.
</correlate>

<correlate>
Heidegger's "poetry first makes language possible; the essence of language must be read out of poetry" is genuinely close to the Indian *sphoṭa* / *dhvani* lineage, where meaning is not assembled from phonemes-as-material but bursts forth (sphuṭ-) as a whole, and where *dhvani* (suggestion) is the soul of poetic language rather than a decoration on top of literal sense. Earned, not vocabulary-deep: both reverse the part→whole, material→meaning priority that calculative/grammatical accounts assume — language's being is disclosive, not compositional. (Caveat: don't overdraw the metaphysics; the convergence is on the *priority* claim, not on a shared ontology of the word.)
</correlate>

<correlate>
The Böhlendorff law — "the ownmost (das Eigene) must be learned as well as the foreign (das Fremde)... what is one's own is most difficult" because it lies too near — is structurally the same move as Merleau-Ponty's: the body/world that is nearest is hardest to thematize precisely because we see *through* it, and self-possession comes only via a detour through the foreign/the other. Non-forced because both make *nearness itself the obstacle to knowing*, and both prescribe an Ausflug, a departure, as the only route back to the proper. (This one is the strongest of the three.)
</correlate>
